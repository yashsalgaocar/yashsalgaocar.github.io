SCR19 Aerodynamics

Trade Study on Downforce, Drag, and Vehicle Mass

Three of the most important factors to consider when designing an aero package in FSAE is downforce, drag, and weight. In order guide design decisions for the aero package, I swept these three paramaters in a lap simulator to determine their affect on lap times. The plot shows an increase in the aero scaling factor (scaling downforce and drag equally) decreases lap times--designing for maximum downforce outweighs designing for aero efficiency (Lift/Drag). Additionally, the slope of the curves shows that a heavy, but high performance aero package would outweigh a light but poor performing aero package i.e. a 74% increase in aero package weight needs only a 12% increase in the aero scaling factor to produce the same lap time. FSAE tracks emphasize cornering over straight line speed, so the cars are almost always grip limited rather than power limited, so it would make sense that drag and vehicle mass are less important.

Front Wing Designs

The primary goal for the front wing for SCR19 is to reduce downforce sensitivity in dynamic conditions of pitch, roll, and yaw. Last year's front wing lost more than half of its downforce with a 1 inch drop in ride height, which was achieved in maximum braking conditions. The SCR19 front wing has a less cambered profile, allowing for a more even low pressure region distributed across the underside of the airfoil in ground effect. Rear Wheel and Suspension Package

This year’s car features 13” magnesium wheels. These wheels maintain wheel stiffness while also reducing a unsprung mass by 3.5 lbm per wheel when compared to aluminum wheels featured last year. This lower unsprung mass increases overall acceleration of the vehicle and reduces wheel vibrations, providing more consistent tyre grip.

Automated Tool Changing Mechanism for the KUKA Robot Arm

As a research assistant at the Center for Advanced Manufacturing this summer, I worked with a team of graduate students on designing a low-cost 3D printed tool changing system for the KUKA iiwa robotic arm. The system will be used for sanding composite structures for a project funded by Lockheed Martin. The tools all have metals plates and will be picked up by the robot using a solenoid core. Solar Air Heater, HAPI Maya Culpa LLC

As a product design engineer at HAPI, I remotely worked on designing a sustainable solar air heater, with the goal of providing heating and ventilation capabilities for controlled environment food production facilities. The preliminary design consisted of an insulated plywood frame with three layers of clear plexiglass. Plexiglass is a good insulator and is also clear, allowing for significant heating by radiation. Fluid simulations were done using Solidworks FlowSimulation and were based on environment conditions in Columbus, Ohio, in Spring. The simulations showed a temperature increase from 20F to 80F in around 9 minutes.
